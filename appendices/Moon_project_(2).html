This study aimed to explain the correlation between youth unemployment and the Gini Index. The scope of the study is from 2004 to 2016, and OECD countries. The concepts of research are as follows: Unemployment rates for people aged 15 to 24 were analyzed through the World Bank Database, and Gini Index was also secured through the World Bank. Gini index counted the economic inequality (income imbalance). It is a statistical index indicating the degree of inequality. Research hypothesis is “There is a positive relationship between youth unemployment and income inequality in OECED countries”

The data analysis results are as follows: The OECD overall trend shows that the correlation trend line slope increased between 2008 and 2010, indicating a general positive relationship between youth unemployment rate and the Gini coefficient. However, between 2004 and 2007, and 2011 and 2016, the slope of the trend line is close to zero. The correlation between the Gini coefficient and the unemployment rate is not high, and the slope decreased in 2016. The relationship between the Gini coefficient and the youth unemployment rate does not manifest clearly, and each year shows different aspects. The Gini coefficient might have greater links with other variables than affect youth unemployment rate.

Let’s look at each continent. In Asia, data analysis is difficult due to the lack of continuous data on the Gini coefficient. However, in 2015, data from relatively many countries were available and the results were as follows: In Asia, youth unemployment rates and the Gini coefficient are positively correlated. In particular, the slope is close to 1, showing a very high level of relationship. The 2015 data from Japan, South Korea, Israel and Turkey fit the research hypothesis.

Europe’s data is similar to the overall OECD trend. However, it shows positive correlation even after 2010. The trend line slope from 2004 to 2007 is close to zero, but since then it shows a steady positive correlation. Data in Europe suggest that unemployment affects the Gini coefficient, and this trend has been in full swing since 2008. In particular, it shows high correlation in central and eastern Europe, including Austria, Greece and Italy.

The North American region is completely different from the research theory. In 2014 and 2016, when data from Mexico, the United States and Canada can all be obtained, there is a very strong negative correlation. This can be said that the higher the unemployment rate, the lower the Gini coefficient, which is completely in contrast to what Europe and Asia look like.

As a result, the research hypothesis established before data analysis could not be clearly confirmed. Although the situation looks different from year to year, in Asia, Europe, Oceania and South America, the correlation between the Gini index and the unemployment rate was confirmed. It is consistent with the research hypothesis in all continents except North America. In addition, we could see that the correlation grew over time. The slope of the trend line increased over time and became more and more connected. This could be said that youth unemployment rate is a major factor in worsening income inequality.

We shall conclude by conveying policy implications. First of all, countries other than North America should pursue job policies to ease income inequality. It is necessary to reduce youth unemployment by increasing jobs and thus lower the Gini coefficient. To encourage young people to getting a job in society, the education system and the re-adjustment of educational curriculum will also provide implications. On the other hand, in North America, other directions contrast to above policies should be made. First of all, states must find factors contributing to income inequality. Wage gaps and differences in education levels could be major factors. Therefore, rather than pushing ahead with policies to address youth unemployment, states will have to push ahead with policies that can overcome other factors and contribute to alleviating income inequality.
